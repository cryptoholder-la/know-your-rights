Common Financial Issues & Disputes

Double Charging / Software Issues

Real Example Scenario:

Property management software erroneously charged a tenant twice monthly for 6 months, totaling $5,000 in overcharges.

Steps to Resolve:

  1. Document all charges with screenshots and bank statements
  2. Send written notice to property management via certified mail
  3. File complaint with Consumer Financial Protection Bureau
  4. Contact bank to dispute unauthorized charges
  5. File small claims court case if not resolved (up to $10,000)

Property Sale to Developers

Scenario: Building Sold to Developer

Your rights when building ownership changes:

  • Existing lease remains valid through term
  • Security deposit must transfer to new owner
  • Written notice of ownership change required
  • RSO protections continue if applicable

Developer Tactics to Watch For:

  • Pressuring buyouts without providing written offers
  • Claiming false "major remodeling" plans
  • Reducing services to force moveouts
  • Harassment through constant construction

Protection Steps:

  1. Document all communication with new owner
  2. Record all construction noise/disruption
  3. Join or form tenant association
  4. File complaint with Housing Department for harassment
  5. Request written buyout offer (required by law)
  6. 30-day cooling off period for any buyout agreement

Common Software/Billing Issues

Documented Cases:

  • Late fees charged despite on-time payment
  • Rent payments applied to wrong unit
  • Multiple charges for single payment
  • Failure to credit payments made

Always Keep:

  • Screenshots of online payments
  • Bank statements showing withdrawals
  • Email confirmations
  • Receipt numbers
  • Names of staff spoken with

Legal Fee Recovery

If you prevail in a lawsuit against your landlord, you may be entitled to:

  • Attorney fees (if lease provides for landlord attorney fees)
  • Statutory damages ($2,000 per harassment incident)
  • Actual damages (money lost due to violations)
  • Triple damages for willful withholding of security deposit

Pasadena Fair and Equitable Housing Charter Amendment

Overview and Purpose

Article XVIII of the Pasadena City Charter establishes comprehensive rent control and tenant protections, including:

  • Rent increase limitations
  • Just cause eviction requirements
  • Tenant relocation assistance
  • Creation of Rental Housing Board
  • Rental Registry requirements

Key Protections

Rent Control

  • Annual rent increases limited to 75% of CPI increase
  • Only one rent increase allowed per 12-month period
  • Base rent established as of May 17, 2021
  • Landlords must register units and rents with Rental Board

Just Cause Eviction Protection

  • Landlords can only evict for specific enumerated reasons
  • Written notice and opportunity to cure required
  • Relocation assistance required for no-fault evictions
  • Additional protections for elderly and disabled tenants

Rental Housing Board

Board Composition

  • 11 total members appointed by City Council
  • 7 must be tenants (one from each district)
  • 4 at-large members
  • 2 alternate members
  • Members serve 4-year staggered terms

Board Powers

  • Set annual rent adjustment amounts
  • Hear petitions for individual rent adjustments
  • Maintain rental registry
  • Issue regulations and enforce ordinance
  • Provide education and outreach

Tenant Rights Under Amendment

Right to Petition

Tenants can petition for rent decreases due to:

  • Reduction in housing services
  • Failure to maintain habitable conditions
  • Unlawful rent charges

Protection from Retaliation

  • Landlords prohibited from harassing tenants
  • No eviction or rent increase in retaliation
  • Right to organize and join tenant associations

What To Do If... (Top 25 Renter Pitfalls)

1. Your Landlord Enters Without Notice

Required: 24-hour written notice except emergencies

  1. Document date/time of unauthorized entry
  2. Send written objection via certified mail
  3. Install legal security camera facing entry
  4. File complaint with local housing authority
  5. Consider restraining order for repeated violations

2. Maintenance Requests Are Ignored

  1. Submit requests in writing, keep copies
  2. Document issues with photos/video
  3. After 30 days, file complaint with housing department
  4. Consider repair-and-deduct remedy
  5. Request housing inspection

3. Mold Is Discovered

  1. Photograph evidence
  2. Request professional inspection
  3. Submit written notice to landlord
  4. Get medical documentation if health affected
  5. Contact health department

4. Rent Is Increased Above Legal Limit

  1. Verify local rent control laws
  2. Calculate allowable increase percentage
  3. Request written justification
  4. File petition with rent board
  5. Consider tenant union support

5. Security Deposit Not Returned

  1. Send demand letter after 21 days
  2. Request itemized deductions
  3. Dispute unreasonable charges
  4. File small claims court case
  5. Seek triple damages if withheld in bad faith

6. Neighbors Creating Excessive Noise

  1. Document incidents with recordings
  2. Report to property management
  3. File police reports for violations
  4. Request mediation services
  5. Break lease for breach of quiet enjoyment

7. Parking Space Is Taken

  1. Document assigned space in lease
  2. Photograph violations
  3. Report to property management
  4. Request parking enforcement
  5. Seek rent reduction for loss of amenity

8. Appliances Stop Working

  1. Report malfunction immediately
  2. Document loss of food/expenses
  3. Request replacement timeline
  4. Seek compensation for losses
  5. File complaint if not resolved

9. Pest Infestation Occurs

  1. Document infestation with photos
  2. Request professional extermination
  3. Keep records of all treatments
  4. Report to health department if needed
  5. Consider temporary relocation

10. Construction Disrupts Living Space

  1. Request construction schedule
  2. Document noise/dust levels
  3. Seek rent reduction during work
  4. Report unsafe conditions
  5. Request alternative accommodation

California Civil Rights Department (CRD) Resources

Housing Discrimination Protections

Protected Categories Under FEHA:

  • Race, color, ancestry, national origin
  • Religion
  • Disability (mental and physical)
  • Sex, gender, gender identity, gender expression
  • Sexual orientation
  • Familial status
  • Source of income
  • Marital status
  • Age
  • Medical condition
  • Genetic information
  • Immigration status

How to File a Discrimination Complaint:

  1. Document all incidents with dates, times, witnesses
  2. Gather evidence (emails, texts, photos, videos)
  3. File complaint within one year of discrimination
  4. Submit through CRD website or call 800-884-1684
  5. Participate in CRD investigation

Common Housing Discrimination Examples

  • Refusing to rent based on protected characteristics
  • Stating preferences in rental listings
  • Different terms, conditions, or privileges
  • Harassment or hostile environment
  • Refusing reasonable accommodations for disabilities
  • Discriminatory statements or advertisements

Definitions and Glossary of Rental Terms

A

Abandonment Notice
A document used to notify a tenant that their tenancy has been terminated.
Affordable Housing
Housing that is affordable to low-income families, including both owned and rental housing.

B

Built-ins
Items permanently attached to a dwelling or building such as appliances, cupboards, and shelving.

C

Cap Rate
Short for capitalization rate, describes the rate of return on investment (ROI) that certain properties are expected to produce.
Capital Improvement
Any permanent alteration or improvement made to a property with the intent to raise its value.
Certificate of Occupancy
A document confirming the property does not have structural issues and is fit for human occupancy.

D

Debt Service
Money required to cover interest and principal payments on a loan or other debt for a real estate property.
Duplex
A multi-family home with two units sharing a common wall, with varying floor plans.

S

Section 8 Housing
A government program enabling low-income families to afford housing in the private market.
Security Deposit
Refundable expenses protecting landlords from financial loss from unpaid rent or property damage.

T

Tenancy
A right granted to temporarily occupy or possess real estate belonging to a landlord, commonly under a lease.
Tenant Improvement
Modifications to a commercial space made specifically for a tenant, distinct from Fitting Out.
Tenant Screening
The process of screening potential tenants to determine if they are suitable for a property.

W

Warranty Deed
Legal document transferring ownership of real property and protecting from title defects.

Z

Zestimate
Zillow's estimated monthly rent price, computed using a proprietary formula.

California Landlord-Tenant Laws

Key Laws and Regulations

  • Cal. Civ Code §§ 1940 – 1954 – Title 5: Hiring of Real Property
  • Cal. Civ Code §§ 1961 – 1962.7 – Title 5: Chapter 4: Identification of Property Owners
  • California Health and Safety Code

Security Deposits

Maximum Amounts

  • Unfurnished: 2 months' rent maximum
  • Furnished: 3 months' rent maximum

Return Timeline

  • Must be returned within 21 days
  • Itemized deductions required if any portion withheld
  • Receipts required for deductions over $125

Rent Laws

Rent Control

California has statewide rent control under AB 1483 Tenant Protection Act

Rent Due Dates & Payments

  • Due at period termination unless otherwise agreed
  • Must accept non-cash payment methods
  • Late fees must be "reasonable" (typically max 5%)
  • 3-day grace period required before late fees

Entry & Notice Requirements

Notice Required

  • 24 hours for normal entry
  • No notice needed for emergencies
  • Entry only during normal business hours

Valid Entry Reasons

  • Necessary repairs/maintenance
  • Showing unit to prospective tenants
  • Inspecting smoke alarms
  • Court-ordered entry

Required Disclosures

  • Bed bug information and reporting procedures
  • Copy of lease within 15 days
  • Utility billing arrangements
  • Mold hazards
  • Demolition permits
  • Lead paint (pre-1978 buildings)
  • Sex offender database notice
  • Death on property disclosure requirements

Eviction Process

Valid Reasons

  • Nonpayment of rent
  • Lease violations
  • Illegal activity
  • Holdover tenancy
  • Property foreclosure

Required Notices

  • 3 days: Nonpayment/lease violations
  • 30/60 days: Month-to-month termination
  • 90 days: Foreclosure eviction
